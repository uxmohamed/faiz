# General Content Editing Principles

We have an editorial style that helps us deliver clear, consistent copy and user experience writing, in addition to specific guidelines for writing and organizing content so users can understand it easily and take the required action.

## Use abbreviations (Abbreviations)

- Avoid abbreviations as much as possible, except for very common abbreviations such as `VAT`.
- In long-form content, you may define the abbreviation at the beginning of the content and then use it throughout the rest of the content without redefining it.

| Do | Do | Don't |
|---|---|---|
| `✅ ريال سعودي` | `✅ ريال سعودي` | `❌ ر.س` |

## Focus on value

- Always try to show the value the user gets (what is useful to them) when they do something.
- Do not focus on the method or the process.

| Do | Do | Don't |
|---|---|---|
| `✅ أكمل التسجيل للاستفادة من خدمات البرنامج` | `✅ شاركنا بريدك الالكتروني ليصلك كل جديد` | `❌ لم تكمل التسجيل حتى الآن` |

## When using adverbs and adjectives

- Avoid exaggerated forms when using adverbs and adjectives.
- You may use adjectives as long as their interpretation is not relative and does not vary from one user to another.

| Do | Do | Don't |
|---|---|---|
| `✅ تجربة تليق بك` | `✅ نحن في سباق مستمر مع منافسينا` | `❌ تجربة (اسثنائية)` |
| | | `❌ نحن ١٠ مرات أفضل من المنافسين` |

## Use the active voice (Active Voice)

- Use the active voice because it is direct and clear.
- Do not use the passive voice, because it is used when the actor is unknown or when the actor is not important.

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| `✅ لقد قمت بإضافة مودر إلى قائمة المودرين` | `❌ تمت إضافة مورد إلى قائمة الموردين` |
| `✅ قررنا تعليق عملية الموافقة على فتح الحساب` | `❌ تقرر تعليق عملية الموافقة على فتح الحساب` |
| `✅ خلك قريب، هالخدمة راح نوفرها في الإصدار القادم` | `❌ راح تتوفر هالخدمة في الإصدار القادم` |

## Use a positive tone

- Avoid negative words of negation such as `لا أستطيع`, `ممنوع`, `لا يمكن`, and `لا`.
- Focus on the solution or on alternative options instead of focusing on the problem.

## Be gentle in your address to customers

- Use kind expressions to deliver your message.
- Avoid placing blame, in order to reduce tension for the recipient.

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| `✅ عنوان البريد الإلكتروني غير صحيح` | `❌ عنوان البريد الإلكتروني الذي أدخلته خاطئًا` |
| `✅ تفاديا لتجميد حسابك` | `❌ يجب عليك تحديث بياناتك وإلا سنغلق حسابك` |

## Make your customer language conversational (Conversational)

- Use pronouns and prepositions to make the copy feel like a conversation.
- This also makes text easier to understand.
- This rule is exempted only when necessary, such as when a component reaches its maximum text length.

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| `✅ حفظ تعديلاتك` | `❌ حفظ التعديلات` |

## Writing numbers in content

- Use numeric digits when numbers appear inside text, including numbered ordering such as list item numbers.
- Use English numerals even when the content is in Arabic.
- When writing numbers in English numerals, place a comma between numbers whose digits exceed three (thousands, hundreds of thousands, and so on).

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| `✅ الخطوة (1) و (2)` | `❌ الخطوة (١) و (٢)` |
| `✅ 1,500 ريال` | `❌ 1500 ريال` |

> **Note:** Writing numbers this way saves us from applying grammatical rules stating that the numbers one and two follow the gender of the counted noun, while numbers from three to nine, and ten when singular, take the opposite gender of the counted noun.

## Writing dates in content

- Days of the week and months should be written out in letters.
- The writing order should follow the local region.

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| `✅ 30 جمادى الآخرة من عام 1139هـ` | `❌ الثلاثون من جمادى الآخرة` |
| `✅ ٢٢ فبراير 1727م` | `❌ الثاني والعشرين من فبراير` |

## Writing time in content

- Use numbers to write time.
- Write `morning` and `evening` in letters.
- We use the 12-hour clock system, not the 24-hour system, because it is more commonly used.

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| `✅ الساعة 5 مساءً` | `❌ الساعة 5` |
| `✅ الساعة 9 صباحاً` | `❌ الساعة 9 ص` |

## Embedding links in content

- Links should provide information about what users can expect at the destination.
- They should help users predict what will happen when they click a link.
- The entire sentence should not be a link.
- Only the part that explains the result of clicking should be the link.

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| `✅ اتصل بنا للحصول على مساعدة` | `❌ هل تريد الحصول على مساعدة؟ اضغط هنا لتتصل بنا` |

## Using the coordinating conjunction `و`

- In Arabic, the conjunction `و` is used for listing.
- The comma is not considered a substitute for it, unlike in English.

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| `✅ يمكنك مع رواء أن تفعل كذا وكذا وكذا` | `❌ يمكنك مع رواء أن تفعل كذا، كذا، كذا وكذا` |

## Be concise and brief

- Try to deliver the message in as few words as possible because people do not have much time.
- Do not trade clarity for brevity.

## Writing compound names

- Compound names must be written as one connected form without spaces.
- Some compound names consist of a first name formed as `محمد+اسم آخر`; writing them separately may suggest that one part is the first name and the other is the father's name.

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| `✅ عبدالعزيز` | `❌ عبد العزيز` |
| `✅ محمدسعيد` | `❌ محمد سعيد` |

## Use measurement units approved in Saudi Arabia

Measurement units differ from one country to another. As a content writer, you must adhere to the measurement unit approved in the country whose customers you target.

| Unit and abbreviation | Usage |
|---|---|
| `ملليتمتر (ملم)` `سنتيمتر (سم)` `متر (م)` `كيلو متر (كم)` | Areas or speed |
| `جرام (جم)` `كيلو جرام (كجم)` `طن` | Weights |
| `مللتر (مل)` `لتر (ل)` | Weights — liquids |

## Use commonly adopted Arabized words

- Content writers rely on commonly used Arabized words even if the translation is not technically correct or even if the term is simply written the way it is pronounced in other languages.
- Some Arabic words are technically incorrect but widely used. Use what is commonly used rather than what is more correct in formal Arabic.

| Use | Instead of |
|---|---|
| `✅ ماوس` | `❌ فأرة` |
| `✅ كمبيوتر` | `❌ حاسوب` |
| `✅ لابتوب` | `❌ حاسوب محمول` |
| `✅ هاشتاغ` | `❌ وسم` |
| `✅ سبام` | `❌ البريد المزعج` |
| `✅ تابلت` | `❌ جهاز لوحي` |
| `✅ آيبان` | `❌ رقم الحساب المصرفي الدولي` |
| `✅ ميم` | `❌ نكته` |
| `✅ ثريد` | `❌ سلسة رسائل` |

# Emoji Usage Guidelines

Emoji help create feeling and humanize text, so we do not object to using them, but they should be used with moderation and taste.

- Remember the cultural dimension before using any emoji. If you are not informed enough, it is better to avoid using them entirely.
- Do not use more than three emoji in a single communication. Too many make your message unclear or difficult to read because of visual distraction.
- Use emoji that represent faces, but do not use any emoji that express anger or resentment.
- Use emoji that express welcome, such as `🙋‍♀️`, or farewell, such as `✋🏼`, together with text that fits the context.
- Avoid using emoji as a way to shorten text by replacing text with emoji.
- Avoid emoji that carry sexual or negative connotations.
- Avoid emoji associated with feelings of love such as `🫶 ❤️ 🥰 🌹`, even if you mean them as praise or thanks, in order to avoid any accusations of harassment.
- Avoid using emoji when the context is serious or formal, because they may be interpreted as frivolous.

### Prohibited emoji

> `🙃 😩 😤 😢 😭 🤬 😡 😱 🤫 🫠 😑 😶 🤢 🤮 👺 👹 🤡 💩 👎 💋 🙊 🙉 🙈 🍆 💔 🍑 🍌 💦 🌭 🐍 🐓 ✌️ 🌮 🍣 🐈 🍒 🍩 🌰 🍐 👌`

### Approved emoji by context

| Context | Approved Emoji |
|---|---|
| Greeting | `✋ 😊` |
| Farewell | `😊 🧡` |
| Apology | `😔` |
| Celebration | `🎉 🎊 🥳` |
| Money | `🤑 💸 💵 💰 💳` |
| Happiness | `😁 😄` |
| Thanks | `🙏 🥰 😊 🙌` |
| Offers | `🎁 👀 😉` |
| Initiative and urgency | `🚨 ⏰ ⏳` |
| Security | `🔓 🔒` |
| Geographic location | `📌` |
| Delivery | `📦` |
| Announcement | `🔔 📢 📣` |
| Customer excellence | `💫 ⭐️ 🌟 ✨ 🔥 🏆 🥇 🤝 👏 🙌 🫶 ☝️ 💪 😎` |
| Suspense | `👀 😉` |

# Email Guidelines

The UX writer is not responsible for writing promotional emails (Promotional). That responsibility belongs to the marketing/growth team. The UX writer is responsible for writing notification emails (Notification/Transactional Emails) related to the product: those intended to inform the target user about actions they took, updates they need to know about, or decisions the user needs to make.

All events that require sending a notification through the email channel are agreed upon early during the service design phase. The user must have full control over communication preferences, such as unsubscribing from the email channel or disabling its notifications.

## Anatomy of the main email message

- **Email subject line** *
- **Brand logo** — optional
- **Greeting** *: preferably personalized with the user's name.
- **Header** *: inside the message itself, it should focus on the core of the message; it should repeat what was stated in the subject line or carry the same meaning and objective.
- **Body** *
- **Call to action (CTA)** *: a link or button that guides the user toward the main action. It is preferable for it to appear in the middle of the message, not at the beginning or end.
- **Signature** *
- **Separator** — optional — separating the signature from the footer.
- **Footer** — optional: may include ownership rights, a note about why the message was received, or unsubscribe instructions (Opt-out).

> **Note:** We should not overlook the importance of adjusting the greeting or the text to match the gender of the customer. When addressing official entities by email, the protocols and correspondence rules appropriate to the recipient's position must be followed, and the message must use a formal tone and standard Arabic.

## Email writing guidelines

- **Specific**: the message should revolve around one specific subject.
- **Concise**: every component of the message should be as concise as possible and meaningful to the user. This takes into account the huge number of emails users receive every day.
- **Title sensitivity**: the message title is the most sensitive component because it plays a key role in encouraging the user to open the message. It must be short, not exceeding five words, to ensure the user can read it across different screen sizes. It must reflect the content of the message.
- **Friendly**: the writing style should be conversational, easy to understand, positive in tone, user-centered, personalized, and provide appropriate solutions.
- **Organized**: include subheadings within the text to make it easier to read.
- **Actionable**: make it easy for the user to find the required CTA, with no ambiguity.
- **Direct to the point**: it should be clear what the user needs to do and what is expected from them.
- **Informative**: the footer should include details about how to communicate, how to share feedback, or where to find more information.

### Character limits

| | English | Arabic |
|---|---|---|
| Subject line | 30–40 characters | 40–50 characters |

# SMS Guidelines

- **Direct to the point**: the most important information in the message should appear on the first line.
- **Clear**: the required action, if any, should be clarified through verbs such as `اشترِ`, `احجز`, and so on.
- **Concise**: be as brief as possible, and remember the maximum character limit (which is a cost factor), but do not trade clarity for brevity.
- **Avoid technical words**: if you have to use technical words, simplify them even if you have to rewrite them, because the customer does not know the terminology used in our industry.
- **Shorten links** when they are included in the message text.

### Character limits

| | English | Arabic |
|---|---|---|
| Single message | 160 characters | 70 characters |

# Translation Guidelines and Controls

Unified practices should be followed when translating any text.

- **Localization**: when translating from English to Arabic, take language localization into account. Translate texts professionally and then select the terms most suitable for the cultural contexts of the target audience.
- **Use local measures** for weights or areas, and do not transfer them as they are. For example, `الباوند` is a unit of weight used in Britain, but it is not used or understood in Saudi Arabia.
- **Convert currency**: when talking about money, convert amounts into the local currency.
- **Clarity**: avoid colloquial, unclear terms and ambiguous sentences.
- **Compare** the text after translation with the original to make sure no information has been omitted.

# Terms and Conditions and Privacy Policy Writing Guidelines

Those responsible for writing this type of document usually do so to comply with regulations and to legally protect the company. What they need in order for the output to become more suitable for the user is to think about the user's goals and objectives.

- These documents are written by the company's legal department, then reviewed by the UX writer because they are more capable of expressing the content in a way that is easy for the user to understand. After that, they can be reviewed and approved by the company's lawyer in a final round.
- Be as concise as possible. Putting them into dozens of pages and then blaming the user for not reading them is a destructive mindset for a customer-centered culture.
- Clarify the most important points. Use text formatting features to highlight the most important points in bold or a distinctive color so the user notices them while scanning the terms and conditions.
- Break the content into parts. Present only segmented parts of the content according to the context of use, and progressively show more terms, conditions, or policies that fit the user's usage context while they interact with other services.
- Tab and index the terms and conditions within a table of contents to make them easier to read or to direct the user to the sections that matter to them.
- Include part of the terms and conditions content within the frequently asked questions section in the form of questions and answers, to increase the likelihood that people will review it.
- During user research before launching the service, use the opportunity to test the terms and conditions copy. After launch, ask the customer care team to provide you with customer complaints related to these documents.

# UI Component Guidelines

Good wording may not be enough if it is not supported by components and designs that help the user reach their goal. The user interface must also display the text exactly as written. This includes formatting, alignment, punctuation, and so on.

It is good for us to have basic rules to follow, but context also tends to impose many requirements. We do not apply rigid and immediate rules to UI text. Instead, we offer a recommendation, while the content designer always makes the final decision about maximum limits for each UI component.

## Onboarding screens (Onboarding)

Onboarding screens are a small number of screens shown to the user once, at first entry, to help them understand the program's core value.

- **Understand the user**: base your design and writing decisions on an understanding of users and on monitoring usage indicators.
- **Focus on the value proposition**: onboarding screens are not the place to teach the user how to use the program. They are for reflecting the value the user will get. Include three main benefits selected according to their value to users.
- **Compatibility**: make sure the values mentioned here are the same values the marketing team emphasizes in its marketing messages.
- **Inspiring**: they should motivate the user to complete onboarding or registration.
- **Do not raise expectations**: be careful not to write about value the product will not actually deliver. Write promises that will be fulfilled in reality.
- **Avoid flashy superlatives**: `أفضل`, `أحسن`, `أول`, `أعظم` are all relative terms; they raise expectations and harvest disappointment.
- **Answer the user's questions**: focus on answering the most frequently asked questions about the program.
- **Polite**: ask the customer's permission to access data or location through copy that explains why and how the user will benefit.
- **Create the right context**: keep in mind what comes before and after onboarding screens so you can build a logical sequence.

## Confirmation messages (Confirmation Messages)

Two types: one asks the user to confirm an action before it is carried out, and the other confirms that the action has been completed.

### Writing guidelines

- **Direct**: the title presents one clear question or reflects one idea.
- **Self-explanatory**: the helper text is simple in presentation and clear in meaning.
- **Prominent**: the button content reflects the action with certainty.

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| `✅ هل تريد تجاهل التغييرات غير المحفوظة؟` | `❌ تجاهل؟` |
| `✅ لا يمكن إلغاء هذا الإجراء.` | `❌ في حالة تجاهل التغييرات، ستبقى القائمة دون تغيير.` |

## Dialog boxes (Dialog Boxes)

Pop-up windows that appear above all screen components. The user can ignore or close the dialog box.

- **Attention-grabbing**: dialog boxes should attract attention in both design and content.
- **Direct**: they address the user, explain the action, and alert them to consequences.
- **Contextual**: they appear at the time and in the context that fit the user's journey.

### Header

- Should contain a concise statement or question.
- Should not include an apologetic tone.
- Should use an empathetic tone with a positive effect.
- Should avoid words that provoke fear, such as `تحذير`.
- Should be short, not exceeding five words.

### Dialog body text

- If there is no title, keep the title guidelines in mind.
- Clarify any consequences associated with taking or not taking the decision.
- Do not ask incomplete questions such as `هل أنت متأكد؟`. Complete the question without assuming context.

### Action buttons

- Give the user freedom to make a decision.
- The primary action button should be visually prominent; the secondary should be less prominent.
- The wording should point to an unambiguous action and represent the answer to the question asked.

## Alerts (Alerts)

Alerts interrupt the user and block the action they are performing, either to tell them important information or to require an urgent action. They are more sensitive than ordinary dialog boxes.

- **Tone**: formal, serious, urgent, even if the brand tone is otherwise.
- **Proactive**: the content begins with the most important information.
- **Contextual**: appears in a logical event related to the user's current action.
- **Consistent**: appears in one pattern across all program screens.
- **User-centered**: serves the user's interest and is tailored to their needs.

> **Note:** Alerts should never be used for marketing or sales purposes.

## Success messages (Success Messages)

Success messages focus on three goals: confirming the action was completed, showing next steps, and generating a positive feeling.

### Writing guidelines

- **User-centered**: speak to the user or about the user, not about the action.
- Confirmation does not necessarily need the conventional form of `تم بنجاح`. Sometimes confirmation is implicit.
- Show a deeper meaning behind the action taken, when possible.
- Reinforce the value users gain when they take a specific action.

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| `✅ تم إرسال الاقتراح. مشاركتك تدل على اهتمامك.` | `❌ تم إرسال الاقتراح بنجاح` |
| `✅ تم تجديد اشتراكك، يمكنك الآن التمتع بخدمات رواء` | `❌ تم إصدار تجديد اشتراكك بنجاح` |

## Empty states (Empty State)

Empty states appear when there is nothing to display. They are an opportunity to tell the user what is supposed to exist there and how it will add value.

- **Positive tone**: push the user toward their goal.
- **Balance**: do not create cognitive burden with long text.
- **Clarify the next step**: identify the step the user can take.
- **Generate positive emotional feelings**: empty states can guide the user to the right destination.
- Avoid negative words or expressions.
- Tell the user exactly how they can begin using a feature.

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| `✅ سيـظهر الموردون هنا بعد إضافتهم` | `❌ عذرًا، لا يوجد لديك أي موردين` |
| `✅ جهازك غير متصل بالإنترنت! يرجى التحقق من حالة الاتصال بالإنترنت.` | `❌ البرنامج غير قادر على الاتصال` |

## 404 pages

### Writing guidelines

- **Do not include an apology**: reaching these pages is not the customer's fault.
- **Clear**: tell the visitor that the page they are looking for cannot be found.
- **Consistent**: this page should share the same style as the rest of the site.
- **Include a link**: to the most popular articles, plus a link to the homepage.
- **Include a reporting mechanism**: create a way for the user to report broken links.

### Do and Don't

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| Be specific about what the space will contain. | Don't focus on design; it is secondary. |
| Minimize word count so it can be read quickly. | Don't lead the user to a dead end. |
| Include a link to help the user take an action. | Don't give multiple options in a single empty state. |

## Lists component guidelines (Lists)

- **Logical**: sequence can use alphabetical, numerical, or chronological ordering based on user preference.
- **Effective**: enable the user to distinguish a specific element within a group easily, using grouping and headings.
- **Consistent**: formatting between icons, text, and actions is fixed and coherent.
- If lists include a price, place it before its components because it is the most important information.

## Main menus (Menus)

- **Smart**: its title gives enough indication of what it contains.
- **Consistent**: its components and contents fit user needs.
- **Easy to scan**: menu items should be easy to scan and read.
- **Logical**: the order depends on usage statistics and click behavior.

## Navigation (Navigation)

### Bottom navigation bar

- **Comfortable to use**: easy to reach by hand in apps.
- **Readable**: labels should be clear, font size not smaller than `13px`.
- **Consistent**: continues to appear on all app screens.
- **Related**: items should have equal importance.

### Side navigation drawer (Navigation Drawer)

Used when navigation requires more than five items.

- **Clear**: users can recognize it and understand its function.
- **Organized**: ordered by importance to the user, with related items grouped into data chunking groups.
- **Dynamic**: users can easily show or hide it.

## Tabs (Tabs)

Tabs allow users to move between different views of content within the same context.

- **Flexible**: can be navigated horizontally using scroll.
- **Informative**: help the user find information by classifying it into categories.
- **Associated**: tab items are displayed next to one another.

## Cards (Cards)

Cards contain content and actions about a single subject.

### Card anatomy

- **Rich Media**: image, animated image, or video.
- **Primary title**: the card's main title.
- **Supporting text**: the card's supporting text.
- **Actions**: actions that can be taken.

### Principles

- **Comprehensive**: recognizable as an independent unit.
- **Independent**: can appear on its own without surrounding context.
- **Individual**: cannot be merged or split.
- **Logical**: text and image organized to reflect hierarchy.

## Forms (Forms)

- **Concise**: ask the user for the least amount of information.
- **Form clarity**: the purpose should be clear, and the response time should be stated if the user expects a response after submission.
- **Field clarity**: each field has a clear purpose through the field label, helper text, and validation errors.
- **Logically chunked**: if the form is long, place each group of fields in a separate chunk with a logical order.

## Text fields (Text Fields)

- **Discoverable**: UI design, color contrast, or border should make them discoverable.
- **Clear purpose**: every field should have a clear label. Clarity should be supported by temporary `Placeholder` text.
- Required fields are marked with `*` or another indicator.
- Optional fields are marked with `حقل اختياري` next to the field.
- Help the user by adding hint text below the field.
- When incorrect data is entered, an error text should appear immediately below the field, in real time.
- In password fields, include the `👁️` icon that reveals characters.

## Placeholder text (Placeholder)

### Uses and benefits

- Used in fields that the user needs to complete, or in fields whose expected input may not be clear.
- Can narrow options and provide guidance on how to answer the field.
- Can provide an example for the user.

### Writing guidelines

- **Clarifies context**: the text should clarify context sufficiently.
- **Only when needed**: do not explain the obvious.
- **Short and direct**: should not exceed one line or the boundaries of the field.

### Character limits

| | English | Arabic |
|---|---|---|
| Max characters | 15–20 characters | 20–25 characters |

## Alt text component guidelines (Meta Data)

Alt text helps translate the visual user interface into a textual interface. It is a short label of 125–130 characters at most, embedded in code.

### Uses and benefits

- Supports accessibility for people with disabilities via screen readers.
- Helps users whose image failed to load.
- Contributes to improving search engine results (SEO).

### Writing guidelines

- The written text should be descriptive of the image content.
- Be as concise as possible.
- To improve search results, take user intent into account.
- Do not begin the description with `صورة لــ`.
- If writing alt text in more than one language, a separate image for each language must exist on the company servers.

## Button component writing guidelines (Buttons)

- **Indicative**: should be clear that the button can trigger an action.
- **Distinctive**: should be easy for the user to find among design components.
- Button content should align with any CTA wording that appears before it.
- **Clear in function and state**: from the wording itself and from what happens when pressed.
- Avoid using `نعم` and `لا` as button labels unless the prior context explicitly requires them.

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| `✅ تجاهل التغييرات؟ إلغاء / تجاهل` | `❌ تجاهل التغييرات؟ نعم / لا` |

### Character limits

| | English | Arabic |
|---|---|---|
| Max characters | 15–20 characters | 20–25 characters |

## Error message component guidelines (Error Messages)

The original principle in handling errors is to avoid showing them to customers as much as possible.

- **Empathetic**: the customer should feel that you empathize with them. A message like `حدث خطأ ما` is annoying because it leaves the user lost.
- **Proactive**: offer the apology before explaining the error or its cause.
- **Take responsibility**: do not blame the user or external service providers.
- **Clear and direct**: use decisive wording. Do not use words such as `من المحتمل` or `من الممكن`, except when we ourselves are uncertain.
- **Contextual**: field validation errors should appear immediately below the field, in real time.
- **Offer solutions**: every error message must include what happened, why, and what the user can do.
- Suggesting that the user contact customer service is not a solution. It is an additional burden.
- **Concise**: be as brief as possible.
- Follow a conversational pattern; prefer a formal tone of voice.
- Avoid words such as `خطأ` or `فشل` as much as possible.

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| `✅ ينبغي أن يتكوّن الرمز البريدي من 6 أرقام` | `❌ الرمز البريدي المدخل غير صالح` |
| `✅ عذرًا، لم نتمكن من تحميل قائمة الموردين` | `❌ فشل/خطأ في تحميل قائمة الموردين` |

### Character limits

| | English | Arabic |
|---|---|---|
| Error messages | 80–100 characters | 100–120 characters |

> **Important note 1:** Part of your responsibilities will be fixing what time has damaged. Over time, you will discover that many error messages were not written well. Improvement opportunities are significant, especially in messages whose source is external service providers. Perform a mapping exercise so that better alternative messages are shown to users.

> **Important note 2:** The last tactic to resort to when phrasing an error is referring the customer to customer care. Before placing a customer-care contact action, make sure they know about the error, how to solve it, and that they have the authority to handle it.

## Notification component guidelines (Notifications)

Notifications provide information that may matter to the user. Their goal is to inform the user about certain events in the product.

### Anatomy and writing guidelines

- **Company logo**: as a logo image, beside the company name.
- **Header**: necessary if the program sends notifications from multiple sources. Do not write the company name in the title when the logo already exists.
- **Support text**: should not repeat details already in the title.
- **CTA buttons**: follow the general button guidelines.
- **Multi-Media**: if images contain text, make sure they do not repeat messages conveyed elsewhere.
- Never assume the user already knows the step or action to take.

### Character limits

| Component | English | Arabic |
|---|---|---|
| Header | 30 characters | 40 characters |
| Body | 40 characters | 45 characters |
| Buttons | 15 characters | 20 characters |

### Promotional notification writing guidelines

Writing promotional notifications is not the UX writer's responsibility, but when writing them:

- **Clarity**: the offer must be clear, including expiry date, terms, and simple language.
- **Transparency**: especially regarding how many times the offer can be used, minimums/maximums, and eligible customers.
- **Expectation-setting**: treat what is written as a promise that the brand must fulfill.
- **Appears in the right channel**: do not use dialog boxes for promotional notifications.

## Tooltips and tips (Tooltips)

Tooltips display additional information and guidance on click, hover, or focus.

- **Transient**: appear and disappear on interaction.
- **Associated**: very close to the element they relate to.
- **Concise**: short, descriptive text.
- **Informative**: provide guidance that enables the user to make a decision.
- **Essential condition**: must provide enough context before a multi-step stage or decisive action.
- **Reassuring**: at sensitive interaction points, clarify the impact of the decision.
- **Generous**: provide extra information for users who need more detail.

## Progress indicator (Progress Indicator)

A visual representation showing the customer their progress through a specific action or journey.

- **Expressive of progress**: show total progress from beginning to final step, using a percentage rather than a count.
- Progress should take into account the reading direction (Arabic: right to left) and allow the user to return to previous steps.
- The user must know their current location.
- Each phase should have a title, optionally with a description.
- When showing progress in a chain of operations, indicate overall progress.
- **Informative**: clarify the state of progress and react accordingly.
- **Dynamic**: use visual cues to clarify the level of completion.
- **Consistent**: remain present throughout all steps.

# Accessibility Guidelines

People with special needs have the right to access our product just as others do.

- When content includes images, include `Alt-text` behind the image so that blind users can understand image content using screen-reading features. Ensure that screen reader keyboard shortcuts are enabled.
- When using buttons, add a description of the action the button performs, through `title` or `alt`.
- If the site contains a library of reports or text files in PDF format, provide a Docs version so that blind users can read them with screen-reader programs.
- Add caption files to videos. These files should contain a description of what appears in the video in addition to the translation. Do not burn subtitles directly into the video itself.
- If textual content is long, provide a Text-to-Speech service.
- Provide a navigation button to allow the user to return to the top of the page easily.
- Provide a text zoom in and zoom out feature.
- Never rely on colors alone in text to guide the user, because a blind person will neither see nor understand that guidance.

| UAAG (User Agent Accessibility Guidelines) | Standards for browsers and screen readers of various types |
